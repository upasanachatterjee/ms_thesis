\section{Topic-Level Results}
\label{sec:topic-level-results}

Having established that community-level aggregation reveals paradigm-specific effects (Section~\ref{sec:community-level-results}), we now present the finer-grained topic-level analysis for completeness. These results are also available for five annotation paradigms: Human, Fine-tuned RoBERTa, GPT-4o-mini (baseline), Fine-tuned GPT-4o-mini, and Llama-3.3-70B-versatile.
\subsection{Multi-Treatment DML Results}
\label{sec:multi-treatment-results}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The multi-treatment sweep fits a single \texttt{LinearDML} model per dataset with with the top 20 topic sentiments (determined by number of articles), treating them as separate treatments, controlling for topic-presence confounders via PCA-compressed $W$. ATEs are estimated on the full corpus, providing substantially higher statistical power than the mediation subsample.

\label{sec:continuous-mode}

Table~\ref{tab:multi-continuous} reports the ATE of a one-standard-deviation increase in each topic's sentiment score, holding all other topic sentiments constant. Of the top 20 topics, nine produce at least one significant effect across the five annotation paradigms; Table~\ref{tab:multi-continuous} presents these nine topics. The full table with all 20 topics and 95\% confidence intervals is reported in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix-community-mediation}, Table~\ref{tab:multi-continuous-full}.

\subsubsection{Cross-Paradigm Agreement and Divergence}
\label{sec:cross-paradigm-agreement}

Seven of the nine significant topics in Table~\ref{tab:multi-continuous} show complete directional agreement across all five paradigms: Donald Trump and Republican Party carry uniformly positive ATEs, while Healthcare, Joe Biden, Barack Obama, Hillary Clinton, and Democratic Party carry uniformly negative ATEs. The directional pattern is interpretable: topics associated with Democratic figures and positions produce negative effects (more positive sentiment $\to$ leftward ideology shift), while topics associated with Republican figures produce positive effects (more positive sentiment $\to$ rightward shift). This partisan alignment holds across all five annotation paradigms.

The remaining two significant topics, White House and Defense and Security, show directional disagreement. For White House, the majority direction is positive (GPT baseline, Llama), but Human, Fine-tuned RoBERTa, and Fine-tuned GPT-4o-mini estimate negative effects; only Llama reaches significance. For Defense and Security, the majority direction is positive (Fine-tuned RoBERTa, GPT baseline, Llama), but Human and Fine-tuned GPT-4o-mini estimate negative effects; again only Llama reaches significance. In both cases, significance is confined to a single paradigm, unlike the partisan-figure topics where significance replicates across all or nearly all paradigms.

Article count does not correlate with effect significance. The three largest topics by article count, Elections ($N = \num{1709}$), Politics ($N = \num{1520}$), and Presidential Elections ($N = \num{1331}$), produce no significant effects under any paradigm, whereas smaller topics such as Democratic Party ($N = 261$) and Hillary Clinton ($N = 279$) are significant across all five. Instead, significance appears to track the polarising quality of the topic: explicitly partisan topics (named political figures and parties) carry large, replicable effects, while broader governance and process topics do not, regardless of sample size.

Table~\ref{tab:multi-nonsig} illustrates this pattern with five representative non-significant topics. All five are among the largest or most thematically salient topics, yet none produces a single significant cell. The contrast with Table~\ref{tab:multi-continuous} is instructive: annotators tend to agree on both direction and significance when a topic is inherently polarising, but disagree on direction when it is not. Presidential Elections and Violence in America show full directional consensus (all positive and all negative, respectively), but with uniformly small magnitudes. Elections and Politics show mixed directionality, with GPT-4o-mini (baseline) and Llama-3.3-70B diverging from the majority negative direction. Russia illustrates a subtler form of disagreement: positive sentiment toward Russia shifts predicted ideology leftward under Human and GPT-4o-mini (baseline) annotations, but rightward under Fine-tuned RoBERTa, Fine-tuned GPT-4o-mini, and Llama-3.3-70B. Because none of these effects reaches significance, it remains unclear whether the split reflects a genuine signal in the data or merely noise amplified by small sample size ($n = 270$). Notably, fine-tuning reversed the direction for GPT-4o-mini, shifting it from a negative to a positive estimate, suggesting that task-specific supervision can alter not only the magnitude but also the sign of downstream causal estimates even when neither estimate is individually significant.

All five paradigms agree on the direction of the Trump-sentiment effect: more positive Trump sentiment is associated with a rightward shift in ideology classification. Four of five paradigms reach 95\% significance under both percentile contrasts; only Human narrowly misses ($\widehat{\text{TE}} = +0.135$, 95\% CI $[-0.001, +0.270]$ for $Q25 \to Q75$). Ordering the paradigms by $|\text{TE}|$ on the $Q25 \to Q75$ contrast yields Llama-3.3-70B ($+0.316$) > GPT-4o-mini baseline ($+0.255$) > Fine-tuned GPT-4o-mini ($+0.158$) > Fine-tuned RoBERTa ($+0.154$) > Human ($+0.135$). The two zero-shot LLM paradigms (Llama and GPT baseline) produce the largest Total Effects, while fine-tuning GPT-4o-mini slightly reduces the effect relative to its baseline ($+0.158$ vs $+0.255$), suggesting that task-specific fine-tuning attenuates, rather than amplifies, the Trump-sentiment signal.

In every paradigm, the direct channel (NDE) carries the dominant share of the Total Effect: $|\text{NDE}| \gg |\text{NIE}|$ for all five annotators on both percentile contrasts. The proportion mediated through Politics sentiment is small where reliable: 12.4\% for Fine-tuned RoBERTa, 10.8\% for Fine-tuned GPT-4o-mini, and 2.4\% for GPT-4o-mini baseline. For Llama-3.3-70B, the NIE is essentially zero ($-0.000$ on $Q25 \to Q75$), indicating that the Trump-sentiment effect operates almost entirely through the direct pathway. This structural pattern, in which the direct channel dominates, is consistent across all paradigms and both percentile contrasts.

\subsection{Topic-Level Cross-Paradigm Comparison}
\label{sec:cross-paradigm}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The multi-treatment results in Tables~\ref{tab:multi-continuous} and~\ref{tab:multi-nonsig} reveal a sharp dichotomy in cross-paradigm behaviour. When a topic produces significant effects, annotators tend to agree: seven of nine significant topics show complete directional consensus, and the two exceptions (White House, Defense and Security) involve significance in only a single paradigm (Llama-3.3-70B). When a topic does not produce significant effects, annotators frequently disagree on direction: Politics, Elections, and Russia all exhibit mixed directionality, with baseline zero-shot models (GPT-4o-mini baseline, Llama-3.3-70B) often diverging from the fine-tuned and human paradigms.

This pattern is consistent with the Trump-mediation results, where all five paradigms agree on a positive Total Effect direction and four of five reach significance. The multi-treatment sweep corroborates this with five-of-five significance on Donald Trump, confirming that the Trump-sentiment signal is the most robust cross-paradigm finding in the topic-level analysis. The magnitude ordering across paradigms is also consistent: Llama-3.3-70B and GPT-4o-mini (baseline) tend to produce the largest effect sizes, while Human annotations produce the smallest, a pattern that holds across all significant topics in Table~\ref{tab:multi-continuous}.

\subsection{Topic-Level Summary}
\label{sec:causal-summary-topic}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The topic-level analyses produce a broad and consistent set of significant findings. In the multi-treatment continuous analysis, the directional structure of these effects is interpretable: topics associated with Democratic figures and positions carry consistently negative ATEs (more positive sentiment $\to$ leftward ideology shift), while topics associated with Republican figures carry positive ATEs. This partisan alignment holds across all five annotation paradigms, indicating that the causal relationship between topic sentiment and ideology classification reflects genuine partisan structure in the corpus rather than annotation artifacts. Notably, article count does not predict significance: the three largest topics by article count (Elections, Politics, Presidential Elections) produce no significant effects, while smaller but more polarising topics such as Democratic Party and Hillary Clinton are significant across all paradigms.

Two further patterns are notable. First, the zero-shot LLM paradigms (Llama-3.3-70B and GPT-4o-mini baseline) consistently produce the largest effect magnitudes, while Human annotations produce the smallest. This suggests that LLM-based ideology labels amplify the partisan signal embedded in topic sentiment relative to human judgments. Second, annotators agree on direction when effects are significant but frequently disagree when they are not (Table~\ref{tab:multi-nonsig}), suggesting that the underlying causal signal is strong enough to override annotation-paradigm differences for inherently polarising topics, while non-polarising topics expose the idiosyncratic feature reliance of each annotation paradigm.
