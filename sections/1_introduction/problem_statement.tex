\section{Problem Statement and Primary Contributions}

This thesis addresses the challenge of automatic political ideology classification at the article level, with particular focus on leveraging metalinguistic features to improve classification performance. The research is motivated by four key limitations in existing work: the lack of comprehensive human baseline studies for article-level political bias classification, the fact that prior work has only established that metalinguistic features classify outlets without systematically integrating them into article-level systems via auxiliary pre-training objectives, insufficient evaluation of modern large language models on this task, and the absence of causal analysis examining how specific thematic and tonal features influence ideological classification.

Specifically, this work makes five primary contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Novel metalinguistic integration} of theme and tone features into article-level political bias classification, demonstrating performance improvements particularly for challenging center-leaning content. Our best model, a continued-pretrained RoBERTa augmented with metalinguistic auxiliary objectives, achieves an F1-Macro of 62.19, a 1.59-point improvement over the previous state-of-the-art POLITICS model \citep{liu2022politics} (60.60),
    \item \textbf{Comprehensive LLM evaluation} of state-of-the-art large language models (including GPT-4o-mini, GPT-5-nano, o3-mini, LLaMA-3.1-8B, and LLaMA-3.3-70B) on political bias classification, examining both zero-shot prompting and fine-tuning approaches,
    \item \textbf{Causal inference analysis} demonstrating that fine-tuned LLMs encode shortcut associations between sentiment and ideology that are invisible to standard F1-based evaluation but detectable through causal probing. We establish this through a topic- and community-level analysis of theme-tone-ideology relationships across five annotation paradigms (human, fine-tuned RoBERTa, zero-shot GPT-4o-mini, fine-tuned GPT-4o-mini, and Llama-3.3-70B), and demonstrate mediation analysis as a diagnostic framework for annotation trustworthiness,
    \item \textbf{Expanded dataset} (AllSidesV2) extending the original AllSides dataset of \citet{baly2020we} from 27,890 articles to 48,290 articles, a 47\% increase in annotated content,
    \item \textbf{Informal human performance baseline} through a controlled annotation study with four participants.
\end{enumerate}
