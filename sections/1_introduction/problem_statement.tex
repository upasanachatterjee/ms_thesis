\section{Problem Statement and Primary Contributions}

This thesis addresses the challenge of automatic political ideology classification at the article level, with particular focus on leveraging metalinguistic features to improve classification performance. The research is motivated by four key limitations in existing work: the lack of comprehensive human baseline studies for article-level political bias classification, limited exploration of metalinguistic features for ideology detection, insufficient evaluation of modern large language models on this task, and the absence of causal analysis examining how specific thematic and tonal features influence ideological classification.

Specifically, this work makes five primary contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Novel metalinguistic integration} of theme and tone features into article-level political bias classification, demonstrating performance improvements particularly for challenging center-leaning content. Our best model achieves an F1-Macro of 62.19, a 1.59-point improvement over the previous state-of-the-art POLITICS model \citep{liu2022politics},
    \item \textbf{Comprehensive LLM evaluation} of state-of-the-art large language models (including GPT-4o-mini, GPT-5-nano, o3-mini, LLaMA-3.1-8B, and LLaMA-3.3-70B) on political bias classification, examining both zero-shot prompting and fine-tuning approaches,
    \item \textbf{Causal inference analysis} of theme-tone-ideology relationships in political text classification at both topic and community levels, comparing human expert judgments with automated system outputs across five annotation paradigms (human, fine-tuned RoBERTa, zero-shot GPT-4o-mini, fine-tuned GPT-4o-mini, and Llama-3.3-70B). Our community-level analysis reveals that fine-tuned LLMs produce significant causal effects absent in human annotations, showing evidence of shortcut learning invisible to standard F1-based evaluation, and demonstrates mediation analysis as a diagnostic framework for annotation trustworthiness,
    \item \textbf{Expanded dataset} with 47\% additional annotated articles over the AllSides dataset released by \citet{baly2020we},
    \item \textbf{Informal human performance baseline} through a controlled annotation study.
\end{enumerate}
