\section{Key Contributions}

This thesis makes five primary contributions. First, we present a \textbf{novel metalinguistic integration} of theme and tone features into article-level political bias classification, demonstrating performance improvements particularly for challenging center-leaning content. Second, we conduct a \textbf{comprehensive LLM evaluation} of state-of-the-art large language models (including GPT-4o-mini, GPT-5-nano, o3-mini, LLaMA-3.1-8B, and LLaMA-3.3-70B) on political bias classification, examining both zero-shot prompting and fine-tuning approaches. Third, we introduce AllSidesV2, an \textbf{expanded dataset} with 47\% additional annotated articles, and establish an informal human performance baseline through a controlled annotation study. Fourth, we develop \textbf{improved continued pre-training techniques} that achieve new state-of-the-art performance on political bias classification while maintaining robustness to source leakage; our best model achieves an F1-Macro of 62.19, a 1.59-point improvement over the previous state-of-the-art POLITICS model \citep{liu2022politics}. Fifth, we provide the first \textbf{causal inference analysis} of theme-tone-ideology relationships in political text classification at both topic and community levels, comparing human expert judgments with automated system outputs across five annotation paradigms (human, fine-tuned RoBERTa, zero-shot GPT-4o-mini, fine-tuned GPT-4o-mini, and Llama-3.3-70B). Our community-level analysis reveals that fine-tuned LLMs produce significant causal effects absent in human annotations---evidence of shortcut learning invisible to standard F1-based evaluation---and demonstrates mediation analysis as a diagnostic framework for annotation trustworthiness.
