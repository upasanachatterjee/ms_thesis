\section{Background and Motivation}

In the current digital landscape, the proliferation of politically biased news content presents substantial challenges for democratic discourse and informed decision-making. The increasing polarization of media sources, combined with algorithmic content curation systems, has created online ecosystems where participants are exposed to ideologically diverse context, yet are becoming politically polarised \citep{flaxman2016filterbubblesechochambers,kitchens2020echochambersfilterbubbles,Garimella_Smith_Weiss_West_2021, cinelli2021echochambereffectsocialmedia}. As people are generally worse at identifying their own biases \citep{wang2020bias} than those of others, this phenomenon raises concerns about how individuals consume, interpret, and share news information.

The automatic detection and classification of political bias in textual content has emerged as a recent research area within natural language processing and computational social science. Such systems have practical applications in media literacy tools, content recommendation algorithms, fact-checking platforms, and journalistic analysis workflows. However, the computational modeling of political ideology remains a challenging problem due to the inherent subjectivity of bias perception, the complex relationship between explicit content and implicit framing, and the need to distinguish between source-level and article-level ideological markers.

Traditional approaches to bias detection have primarily focused on source-level classification, where entire news outlets or authors are categorized according to their general political orientation \citep{baly2020acl, darwish2020aaai, ronnback2025biasoutlets}. While this coarse-grained approach provides useful insights for media analysis, it fails to capture the significant variation in political perspective that can occur within individual articles from the same source. Article-level classification represents a more nuanced and practically valuable approach, enabling fine-grained analysis of ideological content and reducing the risk of overgeneralization based on source reputation alone.

Evidence from prior work motivates theme and tone as the specific auxiliary objectives we investigate in this thesis. Metalinguistic features---properties such as affective tone and thematic content that reflect \textit{how} language is used, not just what is said---have been shown to carry ideological signal. At the source level, \citet{ronnback2025biasoutlets} demonstrated that tone and topic features classify outlet-level political bias at 76\% accuracy against a 45\% majority-class baseline, outperforming models that rely on semantic and syntactic features alone. This finding aligns with cognitive science research establishing that metalinguistic awareness---the capacity to reflect on and manipulate the structural features of language as an object of thought---is foundational to human reading comprehension \citep{tunmer1984development, nagy2007metalinguistic, Zipke10092007}. These results suggest that metalinguistic signals mirror what human annotators draw on when assessing political orientation, and motivate our approach of incorporating theme prediction and tone regression as auxiliary heads during continued pre-training.

We note that the literature uses varying terminology: \citet{ronnback2025biasoutlets}, \citet{baly2020we}, and crowd-sourced resources such as AllSides and MB/FC refer to political ``bias'', while \citet{liu2022politics} use political ``ideology''. Throughout this thesis, when discussing specific works or resources we adopt their own terminology; when describing our own contributions we prefer ``ideology''.

While large language models (LLMs) are increasingly used as proxies for human judgment in social science tasks \citep{wu2023large, horton2023large, li2024frontiers, ma-etal-2025-algorithmic}, \citet{gao2025LLMscaution} caution against treating LLMs as human surrogates, demonstrating that LLM behavior distributions fail to replicate those of humans across multiple domains. This thesis provides an empirical comparison of our continued pre-training approach against LLMs in both zero-shot and fine-tuned settings to characterize this gap concretely, and further examines whether the causal structure of LLM predictions mirrors that of human annotators through the causal analysis framework presented in Chapter~\ref{ch:causal-inference}.
