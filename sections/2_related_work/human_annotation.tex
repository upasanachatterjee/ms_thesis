\section{Human Annotation and Baseline Studies}

The establishment of human performance baselines represents a critical component of bias detection research, yet comprehensive studies remain limited. The Media Bias Identification Corpus (MBIC) by \citet{spinde2021mbic} utilized crowdworkers to identify biased language, achieving only slight to fair inter-annotator agreement (Fleiss's Kappa $\approx 0.21$). In response to these annotation challenges, the Bias Annotations by Experts (BABE) dataset \citep{spinde2021babe} employed expert annotators specifically trained to recognize media bias, resulting in improved but still modest inter-annotator reliability (Krippendorff's Alpha $\approx 0.39$).

These findings suggest that even expert human annotators face considerable challenges in consistently identifying biased content. Moreover, these prior studies primarily focused on bias detection rather than directional classification. This thesis addresses this gap by introducing an informal \emph{lay-annotator} baseline: non-expert annotators, recruited without specialized training, each labeled approximately 100 articles drawn from the AllSides test set, with all source-identifying metadata stripped from the article view so that judgments rest on article content alone. Directional classification (left, center, right) is structurally distinct from binary bias-vs-not-biased annotation: it probes a richer ideological signal than mere ``is this biased?'' detection, and exposes annotator-calibration issues (systematic skew toward the annotator's own ideological vantage point, asymmetric sensitivity to opposing-side cues) that binary studies cannot surface. The lay-annotator design therefore both establishes a non-expert performance floor for directional ideology classification and complements MBIC- and BABE-style binary bias studies by characterizing the difficulty of the directional task itself.
