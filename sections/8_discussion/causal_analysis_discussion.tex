\section{Causal Analysis: Implications and Interpretation}
\label{sec:causal-discussion}

The causal analysis in Chapter~\ref{sec:causal-inference} reveals a consistent pattern: causal analysis applied to Fine-tuned GPT-4o-mini ideology labels surfaces statistically significant sentiment-ideology relationships that are absent when the same analysis is applied to expert human annotations or Fine-tuned RoBERTa predictions. Human and Fine-tuned RoBERTa annotations align closely, producing no significant effects across mediation, CATE, or community-level analyses (Fine-tuned RoBERTa's sole significant finding (Hillary Clinton in the multi-treatment analysis) does not replicate under any other paradigm). As only the ideology labels vary across the datasets, these divergences reflect systematic differences in how LLMs and humans assign ideological stance to article text.

\subsection{Shortcut Learning in Fine-Tuned LLMs}

We interpret this divergence as evidence of shortcut learning: fine-tuning on AllSidesV2 exposes the model to a training distribution in which positive Social Issues framing is statistically correlated with leftward labels, and the model learns this framing pattern as a direct coupling that humans do not exhibit. Section~\ref{sec:shortcut-learning} lays out the three lines of evidence supporting this reading; we do not repeat them here.

\subsection{Implications for LLM Annotation}

A growing line of work explores the potential of LLMs as replacements for human annotators \citep{horton2023large, ma-etal-2025-algorithmic, gao2025LLMscaution}. Our findings caution against this substitution in annotation contexts where downstream causal analyses are planned. A fine-tuned LLM annotator may encode a particular pattern of ideology assignment that does not reflect the full range of human judgments. The Fine-tuned GPT-4o-mini model has internalized a specific annotator profile: one that more strongly associates positive social/cultural sentiment with left-leaning ideology than human annotators do.

If LLM annotations are used as silver-standard training data or as inputs to causal analyses, the spurious pathways learned during fine-tuning will propagate. To make this concrete: consider a researcher analyzing whether healthcare-policy framing causally drives article-level ideology classification. If they use Fine-tuned GPT-4o-mini labels as their outcome variable, they detect a significant Social Issues effect (the community that contains healthcare and adjacent policy topics) and conclude that healthcare framing matters. If they use human labels on exactly the same articles, the effect is null and they conclude that healthcare framing does not matter. The two analyses, run on identical text and identical methodology, lead to opposite policy implications. A researcher who trusts the fine-tuned labels would conclude that geopolitics/foreign affairs framing (Community~9) causally shifts ideology perception; a researcher using human labels would find no such effect. The LLM does not represent the full complexity of human annotator behavior; it represents a compressed mapping that over-weights features salient in its fine-tuning distribution, encoding those correlations as apparent causal pathways.

Critically, this concern is distinct from simple accuracy degradation: it is structurally invisible to output-level evaluation metrics like F1. Higher classification accuracy does not guarantee preservation of causal structure, a finding that should inform how we evaluate LLM annotators for tasks where causal or correlational downstream analyses are planned.

\subsection{Differences Across LLM Families}

The annotators exhibit markedly different causal profiles despite all predicting ideology from the same raw article text, suggesting that architecture and training data differences shape which causal pathways models rely on during inference. Fine-tuned RoBERTa's directional alignment with human annotations across Communities 3, 9, and 11 (documented as a named finding in Section~\ref{sec:community-ate}) is the most striking instance: despite also being fine-tuned on AllSidesV2 ideology labels, RoBERTa does not acquire the spurious sentiment-ideology coupling that the GPT models do. Two non-exclusive mechanisms could explain this. First, the encoder-only architecture may be structurally less prone to encoding shortcut associations than the generative GPT family. Second, the metalinguistic continued-pretraining objective (Chapter~\ref{sec:continued-pretraining}) may regularize the representation space in a way that resists the kind of sentiment-as-proxy shortcut that emerges from naive supervised fine-tuning. We cannot disentangle the two without an ablation we did not run, but the practical implication stands: encoder-based fine-tuning with auxiliary objectives appears to preserve human-aligned causal structure in a way that direct fine-tuning of a generative LLM does not.

Baseline GPT-4o-mini and Fine-tuned GPT-4o-mini share the same underlying architecture, so the divergence between them is attributable entirely to fine-tuning. Baseline GPT-4o-mini produces a significant ATE only for Community~1 (Electoral Politics) and otherwise defaults to a centrist prediction strategy that has not learned the Social Issues shortcut. Fine-tuning corrects this centrist bias but introduces the spurious community-level pathways described above.

It is worth separating two distinct properties of Llama-3.3-70B that the discussion can easily conflate. The first is its low F1 on the directional ideology task (54.61), which is a statement about how often it gets the label right. The second is the systematic attenuation of its causal effect estimates toward zero, which is a statement about the magnitude of the sentiment-to-ideology pathway it encodes. These are independent properties: a model can have low F1 but large causal effects (if its predictions are biased and confidently wrong in a particular direction) or high F1 with near-zero causal effects (if its predictions are well-calibrated and weakly responsive to sentiment variation). Llama-3.3-70B exhibits both low F1 \emph{and} attenuated causal effects, but neither implies the other, and they likely reflect different aspects of the model's behavior rather than a single underlying cause.

Llama-3.3-70B shows no significant effects despite its pronounced leftward prediction distribution. Its community-level ATEs are consistently attenuated toward zero: for example, detecting no effect in Community~1 ($0.000$) where the three other annotators agree on a negative direction, and near-zero effects in Communities 9 ($0.002$) and 11 ($0.007$) where Fine-tuned GPT-4o-mini produces significant results. In the mediation analysis, Llama produces the smallest total effect for the $9 \to 11$ pathway ($-0.007$, compared to $-0.108$ for Fine-tuned GPT) and the widest confidence intervals.

The systematic divergences across LLM families, even when controlling for the same input text and outcome task, suggest that ideology annotation by LLMs is not a uniform operation but varies with model-specific feature reliance patterns. This warrants caution in treating any single LLM as a representative annotator.

\subsection{Mediation Analysis as Diagnostic Framework}

A central challenge in deploying LLMs as annotators is that in-distribution F1 alone does not expose \emph{why} a model produces a given label, and the central claim of this chapter should be stated with that scope. F1 is not the only evaluation tool available: out-of-distribution robustness tests, cross-dataset evaluation, and adversarial probing can sometimes catch the same spurious feature reliance that causal probing catches, and we do not claim otherwise. The novel claim is narrower and more precise: \emph{F1 alone, evaluated in distribution}, cannot detect the kind of shortcut learning we surface here, because it measures only the input--output mapping and not the features the mapping uses. Causal probing complements robustness benchmarks rather than replacing them, and is most useful in exactly the setting we examine here, where in-distribution accuracy is high but the underlying pathway from sentiment to ideology may diverge from the human one. \citet{moraffah2020causal} argue that causal interpretability, understanding decisions in terms of which inputs \textit{caused} which outputs, is essential for building trustworthy ML systems, precisely because correlation-based evaluation leaves spurious feature reliance invisible. Our findings illustrate this gap concretely: the fine-tuned model's F1 score (72.48) offered no signal that its ideology predictions were structured around a spurious direct pathway from Social Issues sentiment.

External causal probing is therefore structurally necessary, not merely complementary to accuracy evaluation. \citet{jin2024largelanguagemodelsinfer} show that LLMs perform near-randomly on tasks requiring pure causal inference from correlational evidence, and \citet{joshi-etal-2024-llms} demonstrate that LLMs are prone to post hoc and ordering fallacies when reasoning about causation. \citet{fu2025correlationcausationanalyzingcausal} further show that standard LLMs rely on spurious correlations in their reasoning processes rather than genuine causal structure, a deficiency that RLVR training partially mitigates but does not eliminate. Taken together, these results imply that LLMs cannot be relied upon to self-report or self-correct the causal basis of their annotations; external causal analysis is required.

Our mediation framework provides one such approach. By holding the input text and causal structure fixed and varying only the annotation paradigm, we isolate differences that are attributable to the annotator rather than the data. This design is consistent with the evaluation principles articulated by \citet{cheng2022evaluation}, who emphasize the need for structured causal evaluation pipelines that go beyond conventional accuracy benchmarks. Applied to annotation auditing, mediation analysis can surface which textual features drive predictions through direct versus mediated pathways, offering a principled basis for trustworthiness assessment that F1 and agreement metrics cannot provide.
