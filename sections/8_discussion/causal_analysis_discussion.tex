\section{Causal Analysis: Implications and Interpretation}
\label{sec:causal-discussion}

The causal analysis in Chapter~\ref{ch:causal-inference} reveals a consistent pattern: causal analysis applied to Fine-tuned GPT-4o-mini ideology labels surfaces statistically significant sentiment-ideology relationships that are absent when the same analysis is applied to expert human annotations or Fine-tuned RoBERTa predictions. Human and Fine-tuned RoBERTa annotations align closely, producing no significant effects across mediation, CATE, or community-level analyses (Fine-tuned RoBERTa's sole significant finding (Hillary Clinton in the multi-treatment analysis) does not replicate under any other paradigm). As only the ideology labels vary across the datasets, these divergences reflect systematic differences in how LLMs and humans assign ideological stance to article text.

\subsection{Shortcut Learning in Fine-Tuned LLMs}

The pattern of results is consistent with shortcut learning: fine-tuning on AllSidesV2 ideology labels exposes the model to a training distribution where positive Social Issues framing is statistically correlated with leftward labels. The model learns to use this framing pattern as a shortcut, producing correct labels within distribution while encoding a direct coupling undetected in human annotations. As \citet{Ly2024shortcutlearningmedicalAI} show in the medical AI domain, models optimized for in-distribution accuracy can internalize hidden spurious associations that only surface under distributional shift or causal probing.

The primary evidence comes from the Community $9 \to 11$ mediation ($N = 139$): Fine-tuned GPT-4o-mini shows a near-total direct effect (NDE $= -0.105$, NIE $= -0.003$, 3.2\% mediated). All annotators point in the same negative direction, but only the fine-tuned model reaches significance, consistent with power limitations at this sample size. Human annotations show no such coupling; their labels are not causally influenced by Community~9 sentiment in either direction, directionally diverging from the fine-tuned model. Llama-3.3-70B produces a near-zero total effect ($-0.007$), consistent with its systematic attenuation pattern across all community-level analyses.

\subsection{Implications for LLM Annotation}

A growing line of work explores the potential of LLMs as replacements for human annotators \citep{horton2023large, ma-etal-2025-algorithmic, gao2025LLMscaution}. Our findings caution against this substitution in annotation contexts where downstream causal analyses are planned. A fine-tuned LLM annotator may encode a particular pattern of ideology assignment that does not reflect the full range of human judgments. The Fine-tuned GPT-4o-mini model has internalized a specific annotator profile: one that more strongly associates positive social/cultural sentiment with left-leaning ideology than human annotators do.

If LLM annotations are used as silver-standard training data or as inputs to causal analyses, the spurious pathways learned during fine-tuning will propagate. A researcher who trusts the fine-tuned labels would conclude that geopolitics/foreign affairs framing (Community~9) causally shifts ideology perception; a researcher using human labels would find no such effect. The LLM does not represent the full complexity of human annotator behavior; it represents a compressed mapping that over-weights features salient in its fine-tuning distribution, encoding those correlations as apparent causal pathways.

Critically, this concern is distinct from simple accuracy degradation: it is structurally invisible to output-level evaluation metrics like F1. Higher classification accuracy does not guarantee preservation of causal structure, a finding that should inform how we evaluate LLM annotators for tasks where causal or correlational downstream analyses are planned.

\subsection{Differences Across LLM Families}

The annotators exhibit markedly different causal profiles despite all predicting ideology from the same raw article text, suggesting that architecture and training data differences shape which causal pathways models rely on during inference. Fine-tuned RoBERTa aligns closely with Human annotations, producing no significant effects across the mediation or CATE analyses and only an isolated, non-replicating Hillary Clinton signal in the multi-treatment analysis. This alignment is notable given that RoBERTa is a fine-tuned model, suggesting that its continued pre-training approach (Section~\ref{sec:continued-pretraining}) does not introduce the same spurious sentiment-ideology coupling as GPT-4o-mini fine-tuning.

Baseline GPT-4o-mini and Fine-tuned GPT-4o-mini share the same underlying architecture, so the divergence between them is attributable entirely to fine-tuning. Baseline GPT-4o-mini produces a significant ATE only for Community~1 (Electoral Politics; ATE $= -0.015$), with no significant effects for Communities 9 or 11 and no mediation effects. This limited sensitivity to electoral framing, combined with its observed centrist prediction tendency, suggests that without fine-tuning, GPT-4o-mini defaults to a moderate prediction strategy that avoids ideological extremes and has not learned the Social Issues shortcut. Fine-tuning corrects some of this centrist bias but introduces the spurious community-level pathways described above.

Llama-3.3-70B shows no significant effects despite its pronounced leftward prediction distribution, consistent with its lower F1 (54.61) and broader prediction variance. Its community-level ATEs are consistently attenuated toward zero: for example, detecting no effect in Community~1 ($0.000$) where the three other annotators agree on a negative direction, and near-zero effects in Communities 9 ($0.002$) and 11 ($0.007$) where Fine-tuned GPT-4o-mini produces significant results. In the mediation analysis, Llama produces the smallest total effect for the $9 \to 11$ pathway ($-0.007$, compared to $-0.108$ for Fine-tuned GPT) and the widest confidence intervals.

The systematic divergences across LLM families, even when controlling for the same input text and outcome task, suggest that ideology annotation by LLMs is not a uniform operation but varies with model-specific feature reliance patterns. This warrants caution in treating any single LLM as a representative annotator.

\subsection{Mediation Analysis as Diagnostic Framework}

A central challenge in deploying LLMs as annotators is that standard output-level metrics do not expose \textit{why} a model produces a given label. \citet{moraffah2020causal} argue that causal interpretability, understanding decisions in terms of which inputs \textit{caused} which outputs, is essential for building trustworthy ML systems, precisely because correlation-based evaluation leaves spurious feature reliance invisible. Our findings illustrate this gap concretely: the fine-tuned model's F1 score (72.48) offered no signal that its ideology predictions were structured around a spurious direct pathway from Social Issues sentiment.

External causal probing is therefore structurally necessary, not merely complementary to accuracy evaluation. \citet{jin2024largelanguagemodelsinfer} show that LLMs perform near-randomly on tasks requiring pure causal inference from correlational evidence, and \citet{joshi-etal-2024-llms} demonstrate that LLMs are prone to post hoc and ordering fallacies when reasoning about causation. \citet{fu2025correlationcausationanalyzingcausal} further show that standard LLMs rely on spurious correlations in their reasoning processes rather than genuine causal structure, a deficiency that RLVR training partially mitigates but does not eliminate. Taken together, these results imply that LLMs cannot be relied upon to self-report or self-correct the causal basis of their annotations; external causal analysis is required.

Our mediation framework provides one such approach. By holding the input text and causal structure fixed and varying only the annotation paradigm, we isolate differences that are attributable to the annotator rather than the data. This design is consistent with the evaluation principles articulated by \citet{cheng2022evaluation}, who emphasize the need for structured causal evaluation pipelines that go beyond conventional accuracy benchmarks. Applied to annotation auditing, mediation analysis can surface which textual features drive predictions through direct versus mediated pathways, offering a principled basis for trustworthiness assessment that F1 and agreement metrics cannot provide.
